1. Executive Summary

This report reads the credit-risk temperature of the Australian economy from public data, so a lender can see which industries and property segments are getting riskier before it shows up in arrears or losses. It covers 18 industries (ANZSIC divisions) and 5 commercial-property segments for Q1 2026, and turns them into ready-to-use PD and LGD stress inputs.

Headline picture: the cash rate is 4.35% (+0.50 points over the past year) and the arrears backdrop is low and improving, so the macro regime is set to 'base'. Against that backdrop, 4 of 18 industries score Elevated on the 1-5 risk scale, and 0 of 5 property segments are in downturn. The downturn-overlay table translates this into stress multipliers — up to 2.00x on PD in a severe scenario — that a pricing or expected-loss model can apply directly.

Macro and downturn overlays are dated 2026-06-12; property-cycle data is dated 2026-02-01. The headline numbers are in Section 2; every figure traces back to a named public source in Sections 3-5.

Macro stress - scope and governance

Illustrative scenario design — not calibrated regulatory stress. Base levels are current values from the named ABS/RBA series; the four CRE variables (commercial-property prices, vacancy, rents, cap rates) and all elasticities are labelled assumptions. The portfolio roll-up is exposure-weighted with no diversification benefit. Reverse stress: the moderate scenario (EL uplift 3.23x) first breaches a 2.0x illustrative appetite ceiling. A bank normally builds separate models per material portfolio or a pooled model with portfolio/sector effects; this layer supplies the macro-credit linkage either consumes.
